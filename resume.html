RESUME

Been a minute since you had to worry about that, huh?

Formatting speaks volumes

  1. If CEOs can have a one-page resume, you sure as hell can
  2. Get rid of the objective statement, that's a cover letter;
    optionally you could consider a "Career Summary" as an elevator pitch
  3. Design for skim-ability:
    1. Simple (not too busy) with attractive formatting
    2. Put the best stuff in each secton at the top: space = importance
      so, put the more important things first and spend more time on them
    3. Make your contact infomation reasonably prominent
    4. Keep work experience reverse-chronological; limit it to 10-15 years
    5. Experience first, education second, relevant key skills and credentials last
    6. Pick a font that's easy on the eyes
  4. Save and distribute your resume as a PDF (with your name in the filename)


Content: out in the wild, tents are a pro not a con

  1. Proofread, then print it out and proofread it again
    1. Then have someone else proofread it
    2. Typos and grammitacl errors make you look like you don't care (or are just an idiot)
    3. See what I mean?      ↖ That looks dumb as hell
  2. Use action verbs: "Resolved questions" rather than "Responsible for resolving questions"
  3. Highlight your accomplisments, not your day-to-day responsibilities
  4. Give specifics (numbers of people, projects, users, dollars, time)
  5. Save the space: cut empty words like "hard worker," "team oriented," and "detail focused"
  6. Ditch hobbies, personal interests, and "References Available Upon Request"
  7. Don't presume to use the same resume for every job or role: customize it
